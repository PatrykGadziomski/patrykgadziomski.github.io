I’m not a book criticm but I do love to read. I’m happy to share some of the books that have truly inspired me, helped me navigate challenges in my work or studies, or simply made a difference in my life. Consider this my personal “recommended reads” list, no reviews, just heartfelt recommendations.

Deep Thinking - Where Machine Intelligence Ends and Human Creativity Begins
Garry Kasparov, Mig Greengard ; [2017]
My recommnedation: This is not the history of chess. Nor is it the history of artificial intelligence. This is the story of human achievement and the behavior between human and machines. While reading this book, I took notes because I immediately had ideas for new research topics, projects and even some fun essays. To be honest: I've forgot almost all of them (which underscores the importance of taking notes). This book was a wellspring of great ideas and inspiration. On one hand, it's the story of one of the greatest chess players of all time. Hearing it from his perspective felt like sitting beside him on a cozy couch, sipping coffee, as he recounted the golden age of human vs. machine chess, his emotions during the final battle against Deep Blue, when the board was in flames (May 1997), and his reactions on absurd, even terrifying, visions of a future where artificial intelligence renders human life meaningless. On the other hand, it's also the story of artificial intelligence and how humanity choose chess as the ultimate intellectual test, until they didn't. You don't have to understand any technical details of artificial intelligence to enjoy this book. Just be curios about the topic. What I loved most was the dynamic between human and machine. Why? Because on one side, we have people who see the potential of machines to take "over the more menial aspects of cognition and elevating our mental lives toward creativity, curiosity, beauty, and joy." On the other side, and there's always another side, we have those who want use artificial intelligence purely to make money, to automate everything, and to make machines do all the work for them. I think we can agree which vision is better. Let's return to the book: An adventure through the history of artificial intelligence, beginning with Turing and the Enigma code, thrilling stories of visits to Oxford, the evolution of chess during Kasparov's reign as world champion, and some meaningful insights into how we should learn and how wem must except to learn. Because if we learn like machines, we're downgrading our potential. We are far more capable than machine and that's something we must never forget, even as we use the latest Large Language Model to write our late-night essay. Remember: It should always be human and machine, not human vs. machine.
